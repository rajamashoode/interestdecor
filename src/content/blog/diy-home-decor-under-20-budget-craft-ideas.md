Here's something the home decor industry doesn't want you to know: most of what makes a room look beautiful costs almost nothing to recreate yourself. That ceramic vase for $45? You can make something better for $4. That woven wall hanging for $80? Thirty minutes and $8 of rope.

DIY home decor isn't about settling for less. It's about making more — more personalised, more intentional, and more satisfying than anything you could buy off a shelf. And when each project costs under $20, experimenting feels genuinely low-risk.

Here are 10 projects that prove beautiful doesn't have to mean expensive.

<span class="article-kicker">The Case For</span>

## Why DIY Home Decor Is Worth Your Time

**It's genuinely cheaper.** Retail markups on home decor are enormous — often 300–500%. Making your own cuts through that entirely.

**It's personalised.** You choose the exact colour, size, and style that fits your space. No compromising because the shop only had it in grey.

**It's satisfying.** There's a real joy in pointing to something in your home and saying "I made that."

**It's flexible.** Don't like how it turned out? Paint over it. Start again. The stakes are low enough that experimenting is actually fun.

**It fills gaps affordably.** New home or rented apartment with bare walls? DIY decor is the fastest, cheapest way to make a space feel lived-in and loved.
</section>

<section class="article-section" id="materials">
<span class="article-kicker">Stock Up First</span>

## Budget Materials to Buy Before You Start

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/budget-materials.webp" alt="Flat lay of budget DIY craft materials including rope, paint, mason jars and wood" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A small craft supply kit covers most of these 10 projects. Buy once, make many.</figcaption>
</figure>

Before jumping into individual projects, grab these basics. They'll cover most of the 10 projects below and cost around $30–40 total — which means each project works out to just $3–4 once you have supplies on hand.

**Paint:** A small set of acrylic craft paints ($6–8). Get white, black, and 2–3 colours you love.

**Brushes:** A basic set of 5 assorted brushes ($4).

**Rope or twine:** Natural jute rope, 3–4mm thickness ($6 for a large roll).

**Hot glue gun + sticks:** Essential for almost every project ($8–10).

**Scissors and craft knife:** You likely already have these.

**Terracotta pots:** Small ones are $1–2 each at garden centres or dollar stores.

**Mason jars:** $1–2 each, or reuse food jars.

**Wooden boards or plaques:** Craft stores sell unfinished wood pieces for $2–4.

**Where to shop:** Dollar stores, charity/thrift shops, craft stores (Hobbycraft, Michaels, Dollar Tree), and hardware stores for rope and wood.
</section>

<span class="article-kicker">Project 01</span>

## Painted Terracotta Pots

**Cost:** $3–6 | **Time:** 30 minutes + drying

Terracotta pots are one of the most underrated craft materials. Plain, they look basic. Painted, they look like something from an expensive boutique.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/painted-terracotta-pots.webp" alt="Beautifully painted terracotta pots in white, blush and sage green on a shelf" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A coat of white paint and a geometric pattern transforms a $1 pot into boutique decor.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Terracotta pots, acrylic paint, brushes, sealer spray (optional)

**How to do it:**
1. Clean and dry your pots completely
2. Paint the outside with a base coat — white or cream works beautifully
3. Let dry fully (30 minutes)
4. Add a second coat if needed
5. Once dry, add a pattern: simple geometric shapes, dipped bottoms, stripes, or abstract brush strokes all look stunning
6. Seal with a clear acrylic spray if the pot will hold a real plant

**Ideas to try:** Half-dipped in terracotta colour, white pot with a thin black rim, sage green with a cream stripe, or a simple abstract brushstroke in gold.

**Display:** Group three different sizes together on a shelf or windowsill. Use them for small succulents, air plants, pencils, or just as decorative objects. <span class="article-kicker">Project 02</span>

## Wall Art From Fabric

**Cost:** $5–12 | **Time:** 20 minutes

This is the easiest wall art hack that nobody talks about enough. Fabric stretched over a canvas or embroidery hoop looks genuinely impressive — and takes almost no skill.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/fabric-wall-art.webp" alt="Fabric stretched in embroidery hoops as wall art in neutral tones" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>Fabric in embroidery hoops makes gallery-worthy wall art for under $5 per piece.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Embroidery hoops ($2–3 each), fabric scraps or remnants, scissors, hot glue

**How to do it:**
1. Cut fabric slightly larger than your hoop
2. Place the inner hoop on a flat surface
3. Lay your fabric over it, pattern side up
4. Press the outer hoop down over the fabric to trap it
5. Pull the fabric edges taut and even, then tighten the screw
6. Trim excess fabric from the back, leaving about 2cm
7. Fold and glue the excess to the back with hot glue for a clean finish

**Best fabrics to use:** Linen, cotton with a subtle pattern, vintage fabric scraps, textured weaves, or even wallpaper samples.

**Display tip:** Create a gallery wall of 5–7 hoops in different sizes. Mix plain textures with subtle patterns. They look incredible grouped together and cost almost nothing. For another budget-friendly gallery wall idea, see our guide to [decorative plate wall ideas](/blog/decorative-plate-wall-ideas/).

<section class="article-section" id="mason-jars">
<span class="article-kicker">Project 03</span>

## Mason Jar Candle Holders

**Cost:** $4–8 | **Time:** 20 minutes

Mason jars are incredibly versatile. Painted or wrapped, they transform into elegant candle holders that look like they belong in a boutique hotel.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/mason-jar-candles.webp" alt="Decorated mason jars as candle holders with twine and paint on a dining table" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>Twine-wrapped mason jars with tea light candles create instant atmosphere for almost nothing.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Mason jars or glass jars, twine or rope, hot glue, paint, tea light candles

**Version 1 — Twine Wrapped:**
1. Apply a line of hot glue around the base of the jar
2. Press the end of your twine into the glue and hold for 10 seconds
3. Continue wrapping upward, adding glue every few rows
4. Cover as much or as little as you like — half-wrapped looks great
5. Trim and glue the end to finish

**Version 2 — Frosted Paint:**
1. Mix white acrylic paint with a little water (50/50)
2. Paint the inside of the jar with the mixture
3. While wet, turn upside down to let excess drip out
4. Let dry fully — creates a beautiful frosted glass effect

**Version 3 — Colour Dipped:**
1. Dip the bottom third of the jar in paint
2. Let drip dry upside down on parchment paper
3. The natural drip lines look artistic and intentional

**Place a tea light or LED candle inside for instant atmosphere.**
</section>

<span class="article-kicker">Project 04</span>

## Rope Baskets

**Cost:** $8–15 | **Time:** 45–60 minutes

Rope baskets are everywhere in home decor stores right now — and they're priced accordingly ($30–60 each). Making your own costs a fraction and takes less than an hour.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/rope-baskets.webp" alt="Handmade rope coil baskets in natural jute in different sizes on a shelf" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>Coiled rope baskets look artisan and expensive — but cost under $10 to make.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Thick jute rope (5–8mm), hot glue gun, scissors

**How to do it:**
1. Start with the base: coil the rope tightly into a flat circle, gluing as you go (apply hot glue to the side of each coil, pressing the next row firmly against it)
2. When the base is the size you want (10–15cm diameter), start building the sides by angling the rope upward slightly with each row
3. Continue coiling and gluing upward until the basket reaches your desired height
4. Trim the rope end at an angle and glue it flush against the basket wall

**Tips for a neat finish:**
- Work slowly and press each coil firmly before moving on
- Keep tension consistent — too loose and it won't hold shape
- For a coloured version, paint the rope before coiling or use coloured cotton rope

**Use them for:** storing throws, holding plants, organising magazines, or purely as decorative objects.
</section>

<section class="article-section" id="string-art">
<span class="article-kicker">Project 05</span>

## String Wall Art

**Cost:** $6–10 | **Time:** 45 minutes

String art sounds complicated but it's surprisingly straightforward — and the results are genuinely stunning. Geometric patterns look modern and intentional on any wall.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/string-wall-art.webp" alt="Geometric string art on a dark wooden board with gold thread on a white wall" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A simple geometric pattern in gold thread on a dark wood board looks incredibly striking.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Wooden board, small nails, hammer, coloured thread or embroidery floss, pencil

**How to do it:**
1. Paint or stain your wooden board and let dry
2. Draw your pattern on paper first — simple shapes work best: triangle, star, diamond, letter, or abstract lines
3. Trace the pattern lightly onto the board with pencil
4. Hammer small nails along the pattern lines, spacing them evenly (about 1–1.5cm apart)
5. Tie your thread to a starting nail and begin weaving: go from nail to nail creating the pattern, wrapping twice around each nail
6. Vary the thread paths to create density and depth
7. Tie off the end and trim

**Easiest patterns for beginners:** A simple triangle, a sunburst from a central point, or the outline of a leaf or mountain shape.

**Thread colours that work beautifully:** Gold on black wood, white on dark walnut, terracotta on cream, or navy on natural pine.
</section>

## Painted Wooden Signs

**Cost:** $4–8 | **Time:** 30 minutes + drying

Personalised wooden signs for the home — the kind that sell for $25–40 in gift shops — take about 30 minutes to make yourself.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/painted-wooden-signs.webp" alt="Hand painted wooden signs with simple words leaning against a wall in neutral tones" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>Simple words in clean lettering on a painted wooden board. Minimalist and timeless.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Wooden board or plaque, paint, fine brush or paint pen, sandpaper

**How to do it:**
1. Lightly sand the wood for a smooth surface
2. Paint a base colour and let dry fully
3. Lightly pencil your word or phrase onto the board
4. Paint over the pencil marks carefully with a fine brush or use a paint pen for cleaner lines
5. Let dry, then erase any visible pencil lines
6. Sand lightly for a vintage, slightly worn look (optional)
7. Add a twine loop at the back for hanging

**Words and phrases that work well:** Home, Gather, Breathe, Create, Simple Living, or just a single initial.

**Style tip:** Keep it simple. One word in clean lettering on a white or black background looks far more elegant than a busy design.
</section>

## Fabric Banner

**Cost:** $3–7 | **Time:** 30 minutes

Fabric or felt banners add colour, texture, and movement to any wall. They're especially beautiful in bedrooms, nurseries, and above mantels.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/fabric-banner.webp" alt="Handmade fabric triangle banner in neutral boho tones hanging on a wall" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A simple triangle pennant banner in neutral linen tones looks effortlessly boho and stylish.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Fabric scraps or felt, twine or thin rope, scissors, hot glue (or needle and thread)

**How to do it:**
1. Cut fabric into triangles — all the same size for a uniform look, or vary them for a more eclectic feel
2. For each triangle, fold the top edge over the twine and glue or sew down
3. Space triangles evenly along the twine as you go
4. Leave extra twine at each end for hanging

**Fabric combinations that look beautiful:**
- All neutral linens (cream, oat, warm white)
- Alternating white and terracotta
- Patterned with plain in the same colour family
- All the same fabric for a minimal, modern look

**Variation:** Instead of triangles, try rectangles with frayed edges for a more organic, boho look.
</section>

## Succulent Planter Display

**Cost:** $5–12 | **Time:** 20 minutes

Succulents are the perfect beginner plant — nearly impossible to kill, beautiful to look at, and endlessly versatile as decor. A creative planter elevates them completely.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/succulent-planter.webp" alt="Creative DIY succulent planters in painted pots and unexpected containers on a windowsill" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>Succulents in unexpected containers — painted cans, vintage cups, tiny terracotta pots — look charming grouped together.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Succulents ($1–3 each from garden centres), creative containers, potting mix, small stones or gravel

**Container ideas that work beautifully:**
- Painted tin cans (paint and seal them first)
- Vintage teacups or small bowls
- Wooden boxes lined with plastic
- Painted terracotta pots (from Project 01!)
- Small glass jars filled with gravel at the base

**How to do it:**
1. Add a layer of small stones or gravel to the bottom for drainage
2. Fill with well-draining succulent or cactus potting mix
3. Make a small hole, insert the succulent, and press soil around it
4. Top with decorative gravel or small stones

**Display idea:** Group 5–7 different succulents in a mix of containers along a windowsill. Vary the heights and textures. It looks like a considered, curated display — but costs almost nothing. Pair your new planters with easy-care greenery from our guide to the [best houseplants for your living room](/blog/best-houseplants-for-living-room/).

## Simple Woven Wall Hanging

**Cost:** $8–15 | **Time:** 60–90 minutes

Woven wall hangings are statement pieces. The ones in shops cost $50–150. A beginner version made at home costs under $15 and looks genuinely beautiful.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/woven-wall-hanging.webp" alt="Simple handmade woven wall hanging in cream and natural tones hanging on a white wall" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A simple woven wall hanging in natural tones adds texture and warmth to any blank wall.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** A wooden dowel or thick branch, assorted yarn in 2–3 colours, scissors, a comb

**How to do it:**
1. Cut lengths of yarn about 3x your desired finished length
2. Fold each piece in half and attach to the dowel using a lark's head knot (fold in half, loop over the dowel, pull the ends through the loop)
3. Fill the dowel with knots until you have your desired width
4. Weave horizontal rows by passing a long piece of yarn over and under alternating strands, pushing each row up tightly with a comb
5. Alternate colours as you work upward for pattern and texture
6. Add fringe at the bottom by leaving yarn ends loose or cutting them to shape
7. Trim the bottom into a V-shape or straight line for a clean finish

**Beginner tip:** Don't aim for perfect. Slight irregularities make handwoven pieces look more artisan and authentic, not worse.
</section>

## Styled Cork Board Bulletin Board

**Cost:** $5–10 | **Time:** 20 minutes

A plain cork board is functional but uninspiring. With a little effort it becomes a stylish feature wall piece — perfect for a home office, kitchen, or bedroom.

<figure class="blog-figure">
  <div class="blog-image-wrap">
    <img src="/images/blog-4-diy-decor/cork-board.webp" alt="Styled cork board with fabric border and decorative pins on a home office wall" loading="lazy" />
  </div>
  <figcaption>A plain cork board transformed with a painted frame and styled with photos, notes, and plants.</figcaption>
</figure>

**What you need:** Cork board ($3–5 from dollar stores), fabric or paint, hot glue, decorative pins or washi tape

**Version 1 — Painted Frame:**
1. Paint the frame of the cork board in a colour that matches your room
2. Add a second coat once dry
3. Optional: paint geometric shapes or a colour block directly onto the cork itself

**Version 2 — Fabric Covered Frame:**
1. Cut fabric to wrap around the frame
2. Hot glue the fabric neatly around the frame edges
3. Miter the corners for a clean finish

**Styling the board:**
- Pin a few postcards or photos
- Add a small air plant or dried flower bunch pinned in the corner
- Use washi tape to create sections
- Pin a small calendar and a few favourite quotes

The goal isn't to cover every inch — negative space makes the board look styled rather than cluttered.

Ten projects, ten chances to make your home more beautiful for less than the cost of a single shop-bought decor piece. Start with whichever one excites you most — there's no wrong order.

Every attempt teaches you something, and the results — imperfections and all — carry something no store-bought piece ever can: the fact that you made it yourself.
